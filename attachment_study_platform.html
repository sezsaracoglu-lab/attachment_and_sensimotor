Schaffer's 4 Phases of Attachment Development
Sequential development of the attachment bond from birth to 2+ years
Phase 1
Preattachment
0 – 2 months
Indiscriminate social responsiveness. Baby responds to all humans similarly. No separation protest. Limited cognitive differentiation between people.
Phase 2
Attachment-in-the-Making
2 – 7 months
Baby recognises familiar caregivers by face, voice, smell. Responds differently to them — but still no separation protest. Perceptual discrimination established; no object permanence yet.
Phase 3
Clear-Cut Attachment
7 – 24 months
Specific attachments form. Separation protest appears (~7–9m). Stranger anxiety begins. Active proximity-seeking. Requires object permanence — infant can now "miss" someone.
Phase 4
Goal-Corrected Partnership
24+ months
Child understands caregivers' goals and plans. Negotiates separations cooperatively. Language replaces proximity. Less distress when reasons are given. Requires theory of mind precursors.

Four Theories of Attachment — At a Glance
Which theory explains what, and how is each criticised?
Psychoanalytic (Freud)
Mechanism: Oral drive gratification — caregiver = pleasure through feeding
❌ Disproved by Harlow (cloth > wire mother) & Schaffer & Emerson (attachment ≠ feeding)
Learning Theory (Dollard/Gewirtz)
Mechanism: Classical/operant conditioning — caregiver reduces hunger = secondary reinforcer
❌ Harlow: food ≠ attachment. Schaffer & Emerson: responsive father > feeding mother
Ethological / Bowlby
Mechanism: Evolutionary — innate signalling system; mutual biological preparedness; IWMs; control systems
✅ Best supported. Cross-cultural evidence; blind/deaf infants social smile; Ainsworth Strange Situation
Cognitive-Developmental (Piaget)
Mechanism: Object permanence prerequisite — infant must know caregiver exists to "miss" them
⚠️ Cannot fully explain onset alone; object permanence not fully present at 7–9m. Useful complement.
Neonatal Reflexes at a Glance
Leman & Bremner (2019) · Permanent vs Temporary reflexes
Permanent Reflexes
Eye blink — bright light → protects eyes
Biceps reflex — tap tendon → short contraction; absent in depressed babies
Withdrawal reflex — prick sole → withdraws foot; bends knee/hip
Temporary Reflexes
Rooting — stroke cheek → turns, opens mouth (→ voluntary ~3-4m)
Sucking — object in mouth → rhythmic suck (disappears ~6m)
Palmar grasp — press palm → grasps (→ voluntary ~3-4m)
Moro — sudden drop → arms out then hug (disappears ~6-7m)
Stepping — upright support → rhythmic steps (disappears ~3-4m)
Babinski — stroke foot → big toe up (disappears ~end year 1)

Sign stimulusLorenz, TinbergenEnvironmental cue that automatically releases FAP; must meet specific criteriaInfant's babyish features (large head, round eyes) and signals act as sign stimuli to elicit adult caretaking

Key statistic: 82% of maltreated infants show disorganised attachment vs 19% of non-maltreated (Carlson et al. 1989). Maternal depression is listed alongside neglect and abuse as a risk factor for disorganised attachment.
Intervention evidence: Van den Boom (1994, Netherlands) — sensitivity training → 68% secure at 12m vs 28% in control group. Non-depressed co-caregiver (e.g. father) can partially mitigate negative effects.
